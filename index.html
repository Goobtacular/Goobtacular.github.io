Early reports first surfaced about a new species of AI that was capable of taking arbitrary text input and generating fairly consistent human-like output. Like many advancements, this was met with a wide array of opinions; Some were excited by the idea of a digital human-like assistant, and some were afraid that we were doing more harm than good by further investing in GAI. That fear and lack of understanding made people angry at the prospect of change. As time passed, the usefulness of GAI was undeniable, and had dramatic effects across all manner of career or business. Suddenly, you didn’t need to do a repetitive accounting sheet, ask someone for help with a review you are behind on, convert hyperlinks into the appropriate reference format, etc. This was incredibly liberating, because we were beginning to replace repetitive menial labour with electronic and robotic alternatives.
The idea was that your department could save a large sum of money on freelancers if you could replace them with a tab in your internet browser. Everything looked amazing on paper; That is, until you actually evaluated the output that these GAI would give you. Most of the time the output was fairly accurate, but there was almost always something.. off about the response you would. The GAI isn’t to blame for this, however. In this nascent form, a typical GAI was akin to a benevolent, omniscient god; One that suffered from frequent hallucinations. The output during hallucination was often bizarre, and mostly guaranteed to be incorrect. Oftentimes, GAI are biased from the misinformed consensus flooding the internet with their views, and act in unpredictable ways when presented with conflicting information.
In order to understand why that was, we had to understand what GAI was built from. They were built upon Large Language Models that need incomprehensible amounts of data in order to develop themselves. The first LLMs scraped vast amounts of online data with impunity, giving them an incredible amount of data with which to train without the knowledge or consent of the owners of the scraped websites. This included style guides, published articles, and proprietary information. In the face of all that information, there were always contradictory opinions for every topic. Trying to rely on their output at face value was destructive to the editor, the body of work, and most importantly, the reader. Many important details were lost to the GAI by virtue of being based on data instead of human experience. It was necessary to have a human element in the process, from proofreading to typesetting; Even when all it consisted of was supervising the autonomous processing of whatever task we gave to whatever specialised GAI we developed.
